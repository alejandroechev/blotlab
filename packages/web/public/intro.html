What is Western Blotting?

Western blotting is a laboratory technique used to detect and measure specific proteins in a biological sample. Proteins are the molecular machines of cells ‚Äî they carry out nearly every function, from structural support to catalyzing chemical reactions. Researchers often need to know how much of a particular protein is present in different samples (e.g., healthy vs. diseased tissue, treated vs. untreated cells).

How the technique works

  1. Separate ‚Äî Proteins are extracted from cells and loaded into lanes of a polyacrylamide gel. An electric field pulls them through the gel, separating them by size. Smaller proteins travel farther.
  2. Transfer ‚Äî The separated proteins are transferred (blotted) from the gel onto a membrane (nitrocellulose or PVDF), preserving their positions.
  3. Probe ‚Äî The membrane is incubated with an antibody that binds specifically to the protein of interest. A secondary antibody, linked to an enzyme or fluorescent tag, makes the target visible.
  4. Detect ‚Äî The membrane is imaged (chemiluminescence, fluorescence, or colorimetric). The result is a gel image showing dark or bright bands at the positions where the target protein is present.

The intensity of each band reflects the amount of protein ‚Äî darker or brighter bands mean more protein. Densitometry is the process of measuring those intensities numerically so researchers can make quantitative comparisons.

How Densitometry Works

Turning a blot image into reliable numbers involves several image-processing steps. BlotLab automates them all in the browser.

Rolling Ball Background Subtraction

Blot images have uneven background intensity caused by lighting variation, membrane staining, and other artifacts. The rolling ball algorithm estimates the background by "rolling" a virtual sphere under the intensity surface of the image, like rolling a ball along the underside of a landscape. Points the ball can touch become the estimated background.

corrected(x,y) = original(x,y) ‚àí background(x,y)

The ball radius controls sensitivity: a larger radius treats more of the image as signal; a smaller radius removes more local background. The default in BlotLab is 50 pixels.

Lane Detection

Each lane corresponds to one sample loaded on the gel. BlotLab detects lanes automatically by computing the vertical intensity projection ‚Äî summing pixel intensities along each column of the image. Peaks in this projection correspond to lanes; valleys correspond to gaps between lanes.

Band ROI Detection

Within each lane, BlotLab computes a horizontal intensity profile (summing pixels along each row). Peaks in this profile indicate bands. A rectangular Region of Interest (ROI) is placed around each detected band.

Integrated Intensity

The quantitative signal for each band is the integrated intensity: the sum of all pixel values within its ROI after background subtraction.

I = Œ£ (pixel values within ROI)

This single number represents the relative amount of protein in that band.

Key Concepts

Normalization to a Loading Control

The amount of protein loaded into each lane is never perfectly equal. To correct for this, researchers also probe for a loading control ‚Äî a "housekeeping" protein (such as GAPDH, Œ≤-actin, or tubulin) that is expressed at roughly constant levels in all samples.

normalized = target_band_intensity / loading_control_intensity

Dividing the target intensity by the loading-control intensity in the same lane corrects for unequal loading and allows fair comparison across lanes.

Fold Change

Once normalized, results are often expressed as fold change relative to a control lane (e.g., untreated sample). A fold change of 2.0 means twice as much protein as the control.

fold change = normalized_sample / normalized_control

Why This Matters

Without normalization and fold-change calculation, differences in loading would be mistaken for differences in protein expression. Proper densitometry turns a subjective visual impression into a reproducible, publishable number.

Glossary

  • Band ‚Äî A dark or bright stripe on a western blot image indicating the presence of a specific protein at a particular molecular weight.
  • Densitometry ‚Äî The quantitative measurement of optical density or pixel intensity in an image, used to determine relative protein amounts.
  • Fold change ‚Äî The ratio of a sample's normalized intensity to a control sample's normalized intensity. Indicates how much more or less protein is present.
  • Housekeeping protein ‚Äî A protein (e.g., GAPDH, Œ≤-actin, tubulin) expressed at relatively constant levels in all samples, used as an internal loading reference.
  • Integrated intensity ‚Äî The sum of all pixel values within a region of interest, representing total signal from a band.
  • Lane ‚Äî A vertical column in a gel corresponding to one loaded sample.
  • Loading control ‚Äî A reference band used to normalize for differences in total protein loaded per lane.
  • Normalization ‚Äî Dividing target band intensity by loading-control band intensity to correct for unequal loading.
  • ROI (Region of Interest) ‚Äî A rectangular area drawn around a band for intensity measurement.
  • Rolling ball algorithm ‚Äî A morphological background-estimation method that simulates rolling a sphere under the intensity surface to determine local background levels.
